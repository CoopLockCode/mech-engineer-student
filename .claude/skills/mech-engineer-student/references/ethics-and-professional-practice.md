# Engineering Ethics & Professional Practice Reference

NSPE Code of Ethics, professional licensure, conflicts of interest, IP, liability, and the engineer's duty to public safety.

---

## 0. How to Use This Reference

Ethics & Professional Practice is its own FE-exam section (typically 3–5 questions on the Mechanical FE, more on some disciplines) and is the foundation for everything you'll do as a practicing or licensed engineer. The FE-style ethics question is almost never a moral puzzle — it is a *recognition test*: "which canon controls this scenario, and what does the canon require?" Memorize the canons, internalize the hierarchy, and the questions become near-mechanical.

This reference is built around the **NSPE Code of Ethics for Engineers** (the canonical text adopted in 1946 and continuously updated) and the **NCEES Model Rules of Professional Conduct** (the licensure-board-facing version). The two documents are nearly identical in spirit; the NSPE Code is what FE/PE exams quote, and the NCEES Model Rules are what your state licensure board will discipline you under.

## 1. The NSPE Code of Ethics for Engineers

### 1.1 Preamble (paraphrased from NSPE)

Engineering is a learned profession whose work directly and vitally affects the quality of life for all people. The services provided by engineers require honesty, impartiality, fairness, and equity, and must be dedicated to the protection of the public health, safety, and welfare. Engineers must perform under a standard of professional behavior that requires adherence to the highest principles of ethical conduct.

(The NSPE preamble is short and unambiguous: it frames the entire Code in terms of public protection. Every later canon is read in light of this opening.)

### 1.2 The Six Fundamental Canons (verbatim)

> Engineers, in the fulfillment of their professional duties, shall:
>
> 1. **Hold paramount the safety, health, and welfare of the public.**
> 2. **Perform services only in areas of their competence.**
> 3. **Issue public statements only in an objective and truthful manner.**
> 4. **Act for each employer or client as faithful agents or trustees.**
> 5. **Avoid deceptive acts.**
> 6. **Conduct themselves honorably, responsibly, ethically, and lawfully so as to enhance the honor, reputation, and usefulness of the profession.**

Memorize the verbs and the targets:

| # | Verb | Primary target |
|---|------|----------------|
| 1 | Hold paramount | Public safety, health, welfare |
| 2 | Perform | Only within competence |
| 3 | Issue | Only objective/truthful public statements |
| 4 | Act | Faithful agent/trustee to employer/client |
| 5 | Avoid | Deceptive acts |
| 6 | Conduct | Honor of the profession |

### 1.3 Rules of Practice (the operational expansions of Canons 1–5)

The NSPE Rules of Practice take each canon and give it teeth. The key operational rules every engineer must know:

**On Canon 1 — Hold paramount:**
- If your engineering judgment is overruled under circumstances that endanger life or property, **you must notify your employer/client and any other appropriate authority.**
- You shall approve only those engineering documents that are in conformity with applicable standards.
- You shall not reveal confidential facts/data/information without prior consent (except as required by law or the Code itself — the public-safety override).
- You shall not permit the use of your name or associate in business ventures with anyone you believe is engaged in fraudulent or dishonest enterprise.
- **You shall not aid or abet the unlawful practice of engineering by a person or firm.**
- Engineers having knowledge of any alleged violation of this Code shall report it to the proper authorities and cooperate with them.

**On Canon 2 — Competence:**
- Undertake assignments only when qualified by **education or experience** in the specific technical fields involved.
- **Shall not affix your signature to any plans or documents dealing with subject matter in which you lack competence, nor to any plan or document not prepared under your direction and control.**
- May accept assignments and assume responsibility for coordination of an entire project provided that each technical segment is signed and sealed by the qualified engineers who prepared that segment.

**On Canon 3 — Public statements:**
- Be objective and truthful in professional reports, statements, or testimony, and include all relevant and pertinent information.
- May express public opinions on technical subjects only when those opinions are founded on adequate knowledge of the facts and on competent evaluation.
- Shall issue no statements, criticisms, or arguments on technical matters that are inspired or paid for by interested parties unless you have prefaced your comments by **explicitly identifying the interested parties on whose behalf you are speaking, and by revealing the existence of any interest you may have in the matters.**

**On Canon 4 — Faithful agent:**
- Disclose all known or potential conflicts of interest that could influence or appear to influence your judgment or quality of services.
- Shall not accept compensation, financial or otherwise, from more than one party for services on the same project, or for services pertaining to the same project, unless the circumstances are fully disclosed and agreed to by all interested parties.
- Shall not solicit or accept financial or other valuable consideration, directly or indirectly, from outside agents in connection with the work for which you are responsible.
- When in public service as a member, advisor, or employee of a governmental or quasi-governmental body, shall not participate in decisions with respect to services solicited or provided by you or your organization in private or public engineering practice.
- Shall not solicit or accept a contract from a governmental body on which a principal or officer of your organization serves as a member.

**On Canon 5 — Honest solicitation:**
- Shall not falsify your qualifications or permit misrepresentation of yours or your associates' qualifications.
- Shall not offer, give, solicit, or receive, either directly or indirectly, any contribution to influence the award of a contract by public authority, or which may be reasonably construed by the public as having the effect or intent of influencing the awarding of a contract.

### 1.4 Professional Obligations (the expansion of Canon 6 and the spirit of the Code)

These nine obligations (paraphrased) are where Canon 6 lives. They are also the source of most "subtle" FE/PE ethics questions:

1. **Be guided in all professional relations by the highest standards of honesty and integrity.** Acknowledge errors; do not distort or alter facts; advise clients/employers when you believe a project will not be successful.
2. **At all times strive to serve the public interest** — including participating in civic affairs, extending public knowledge of engineering, and not completing/signing/sealing work that is not in conformity with applicable engineering standards.
3. **Avoid all conduct or practice that deceives the public** — including any misleading statement, exaggeration, or unjustified claim regarding qualifications, partners, past assignments, or accomplishments (this includes brochures and advertising).
4. **Not disclose, without consent, confidential information** concerning the business affairs or technical processes of any present or former client, employer, or public body on which you serve.
5. **Not be influenced in your professional duties by conflicting interests** — do not accept commissions or allowances directly or indirectly from contractors or other parties dealing with clients/employers on your work.
6. **Not attempt to obtain employment or advancement or professional engagements by untruthfully criticizing other engineers**, or by other improper or questionable methods.
7. **Not attempt to injure, maliciously or falsely, directly or indirectly, the professional reputation, prospects, practice, or employment of other engineers**, and shall not indiscriminately criticize their work.
8. **Accept personal responsibility for your professional activities** — but may seek indemnification for services arising out of practice for which you are not responsible.
9. **Give credit for engineering work to those to whom credit is due, and recognize the proprietary interests of others.** Names of those responsible for design/inventions/writings should be cited. Designs, data, records, and notes referring exclusively to an employer's work are the employer's property; the employer must indemnify the engineer for use beyond the original purpose.

## 2. The Hierarchy of Ethical Obligations

The single most important fact in engineering ethics:

> **Canon 1 outranks every other canon, every contract, every employment relationship, every NDA, every loyalty obligation.**

The phrasing "hold paramount" is not decorative. "Paramount" = supreme, above all others. When public safety conflicts with any other duty — to your employer, to your client, to confidentiality, to a deadline, to your career, to a contract — public safety wins. Period.

The hierarchy (in priority order):

1. **Public safety, health, and welfare** (Canon 1) — overrides everything below.
2. **Honesty in public communication** (Canon 3) — you cannot let lies endanger the public, even by silence.
3. **Faithful agent to employer/client** (Canon 4) — *subordinate to 1*. You owe loyalty UNTIL loyalty would harm the public.
4. **Competence boundary** (Canon 2) — never practice where you cannot perform safely; this is also a public-protection canon dressed as a competence rule.
5. **Honor of the profession** (Canon 6) — the residual duty.

### The Engineer-as-Public-Protector role

Doctors are bound to their patients. Lawyers are bound to their clients. **Engineers are bound to the public** — even the public who does not know your name, did not hire you, and is not in the room. The public uses the bridge, drinks the water, drives the car, flies in the plane. They have no voice in the design meeting; you are their representative.

This is why "I was just following orders" is never a valid engineering ethics defense, and why "my boss told me to sign off" never absolves you. Your seal is your voice; the public is your client of last resort.

## 3. Common Ethical Scenarios — Canonical Answers

### 3.1 The FE-style cheat-sheet

These are the patterns. The "correct answer" on standardized ethics questions is almost always:

| Scenario pattern | Almost always correct answer |
|---|---|
| Public safety vs. confidentiality | **Notify the proper authority.** Safety wins. |
| Boss overrules your safety judgment | **Document your objection in writing; if still endangered, report to authority.** |
| Asked to sign work outside your competence | **Refuse to sign.** Bring in a qualified engineer to seal that segment. |
| Asked to sign work prepared by someone not under your direction | **Refuse.** Only seal work you prepared or that was prepared under your direct supervision and control. |
| Offered a gift from a vendor/contractor | **Decline anything beyond trivial value.** Disclose to employer. The safe answer is always *no*. |
| Two paying parties on the same project | **Disclose to both and obtain written consent from all parties.** If you can't get consent, decline one. |
| Conflict of interest (real, apparent, or potential) | **Disclose in writing and recuse from the decision** if disclosure isn't enough. |
| Public statement on a paid topic | **Disclose who's paying and what your interest is.** Hidden advocacy is forbidden. |
| You discover an error in already-issued work | **Disclose to the affected parties immediately.** You owe correction. |
| You discover a competitor's unsafe design | **Report to appropriate authority** — not to media, not to public — appropriate professional/regulatory channel. |
| Advertising or solicitation | **Truthful, dignified, not misleading.** No exaggerated claims, no untruthful criticism of others. |
| Foreign country asks for a "facilitation payment" | **Default: refuse and check FCPA counsel.** Treat all such requests as bribes unless cleared. |
| Asked to claim credit for work you did not do | **Refuse.** Cite contributors; don't claim solo credit. |
| Asked to work in a state where you're not licensed (and your project requires it) | **Don't.** Get comity/reciprocity or partner with a locally licensed PE. |

### 3.2 Gift acceptance — the bright-line rules

- **Truly trivial**: a vendor pen, a candy bar at a trade-show booth, a calendar — *generally* acceptable.
- **Anything of meaningful value**: dinner past a modest threshold, sports tickets, travel, weekend trips, gift cards, equipment, expensive bottles — **decline or disclose and obtain written employer approval**.
- **Anything offered in connection with a pending decision you control** — **always decline**, regardless of value. The appearance is the violation.

The publication-quality test: if it would embarrass your employer to read about on the front page of a paper, don't accept it.

### 3.3 Signing/sealing only your own work

You may seal only:
- Work you personally prepared, OR
- Work prepared by others under your direct supervision and control.

You may not seal:
- Work prepared by another engineer (even a colleague) merely handed to you to stamp ("plan stamping" / "rubber stamping").
- Work in a discipline outside your competence.
- Work in a state where you are not licensed (unless under a comity arrangement and signed by the correct PE).

Plan-stamping is one of the most commonly disciplined offenses by state boards — it is per se grounds for license suspension or revocation in nearly every jurisdiction.

### 3.4 Working outside your area of competence

The Canon 2 rule has a sharper edge than most students realize. "Competence" means **education AND experience** in the specific technical field. A mechanical PE is not automatically competent to seal structural drawings, even if they took a strength-of-materials course in undergrad.

Three options when an assignment exceeds your competence:
1. **Refuse it** outright.
2. **Acquire the competence** legitimately (further training, mentorship, time) before signing.
3. **Bring in a qualified specialist** to design and seal the specific segment; you may coordinate the overall project.

The danger zone: "you don't know what you don't know." Many engineering disasters trace to a competent engineer extending their judgment one discipline too far. *If you can't articulate the failure modes of the system you're about to seal, you aren't competent on it.*

## 4. Whistleblowing & Public Safety

### 4.1 The duty to act

If you observe a condition that endangers the public (a design flaw, a manufacturing defect, a substandard material, a falsified test report), your duty is not optional. Canon 1 and the Rules of Practice impose an **affirmative obligation to report.**

The escalation pathway (try in order):

1. **Internal — to your supervisor or project engineer.** In writing. With specifics. Keep a copy.
2. **Internal — higher up.** If your immediate supervisor will not act, escalate within the organization. Document the response (or non-response).
3. **External — to the responsible authority.** State licensure board, OSHA, EPA, FAA, NHTSA, NRC, CPSC — depending on the matter. Regulatory bodies, not the media, are the appropriate first external escalation.
4. **External — public disclosure** is the last resort, used only after internal and regulatory paths are exhausted and the danger is ongoing.

You are not required to *fix* the problem yourself, only to surface it to people who can. But you must surface it, in writing, with evidence, and you must continue to push until the danger is addressed.

### 4.2 Real cases — short ethical sidebars

#### Challenger O-rings (1986)
Morton Thiokol engineer **Roger Boisjoly** spent six months before the launch warning that the SRB O-rings could fail in cold temperatures, including a 1985 memo predicting "catastrophe of the highest order — loss of human life." The night before launch, Boisjoly and four other Thiokol engineers urged a launch delay. Management overruled them ("take off your engineering hat and put on your management hat"), and Challenger broke apart 73 seconds after liftoff, killing all seven crew. Boisjoly later testified to the Rogers Commission and was effectively forced out of Thiokol. He spent the rest of his career speaking on engineering ethics. **The ethical question this case asks every engineer: when management overrules your safety judgment, do you have the documentation and the will to escalate?**

#### Hyatt Regency walkway collapse (1981)
A late-stage design change doubled the load on the supporting rod connection of the second/fourth-floor skywalks at the Kansas City Hyatt Regency. The change was approved without rigorous re-analysis. On July 17, 1981, the walkways collapsed onto a crowded atrium, killing 114 and injuring 216. Engineers **Daniel M. Duncan and Jack D. Gillum** were found guilty by the Missouri licensing board of "gross negligence, incompetence, misconduct, and unprofessional conduct in the practice of engineering." Both lost their PE licenses in Missouri and Texas; the firm's certificate of authority was revoked. **The lesson: every shop-drawing change is an engineering decision. There is no such thing as a "minor" modification to a load-bearing connection. Canon 2 (competence) and Canon 1 (public safety) demand re-analysis whenever a load path changes.**

#### Citicorp Tower (1978)
**William LeMessurier** designed Citicorp Center in New York with a novel structural system. A college student called to question the building's behavior under quartering winds. LeMessurier reviewed and discovered that the contractor had substituted bolted joints for welded joints (approved by his firm without his personal review), and the bolted connections were inadequate for the wind loads — giving the building roughly a 1-in-16 chance of collapse in a hurricane season. LeMessurier disclosed the flaw to his insurer, his client, and city authorities, and the building was secretly retrofitted with welded plates overnight over the next several months. No one was killed; the public learned of the crisis only in 1995. **LeMessurier is often cited as the model of ethical disclosure under Canon 1: he chose career risk, financial exposure, and reputational risk over silence. The case is contested in modern ethics literature — some argue the quiet repair was itself a partial concealment from the public — but the disclosure to the responsible authorities is the textbook right answer.**

#### Ford Pinto memo (1970s)
Ford engineers prepared an internal cost-benefit analysis weighing the cost of fixing a known fuel-tank vulnerability (~$11/car) against projected lawsuit settlements from burn deaths and injuries (using a then-government figure of ~$200,000/life). The analysis concluded the safety modification "wasn't cost-effective." Hundreds of fire deaths followed. *Mother Jones* exposed the memo in 1977. **The ethics: an engineer assigning a dollar value to a foreseeable death and choosing the cheaper path has not "calculated tradeoffs" — they have abdicated Canon 1. Cost-benefit analysis is a legitimate engineering tool for design alternatives; it is not a license to ship a known life-safety defect.**

## 5. Conflicts of Interest

### 5.1 The three flavors

- **Real (actual) COI** — your interest and your duty actually conflict (you own stock in a vendor whose bid you are evaluating).
- **Apparent COI** — a reasonable outside observer would suspect a conflict, even if none exists (your brother-in-law is a partner in the bidder).
- **Potential COI** — circumstances that could develop into a real or apparent conflict (you are negotiating future employment with a current vendor).

**All three require disclosure.** Disclosure is the universal first move. Whether you must also recuse depends on the severity of the conflict and the discretion of the employer/client given full information.

### 5.2 Specific patterns

- **Gifts from suppliers** — see §3.2.
- **Consulting on the side** — generally permitted, but requires written employer consent if there is any overlap of subject matter, customers, or time; never use employer resources or confidential information.
- **Dual employment** — usually disclosed; usually prohibited if both employers might call on you for the same project, and forbidden under Canon 4 unless both consent in writing.
- **Public service** — if you sit on a city planning commission or a governmental advisory body, you cannot participate in any decision regarding services your private firm provides or seeks.
- **Family/financial interests in bidders** — must be disclosed; you must recuse from evaluation.

### 5.3 The "no surprises" rule

The bright line on COIs is: **never let your employer/client discover the conflict from someone else.** Disclosure is cheap; concealment is career-ending. The Code does not require you to live without conflicts — it requires you to surface them and let the affected parties decide.

## 6. Licensure & Professional Registration

### 6.1 The path

1. **Earn an ABET-accredited engineering degree** (BS, four years).
2. **Pass the FE exam** (Fundamentals of Engineering) — administered by NCEES. Upon passing and applying to a state board, you become an **EIT** (Engineer-in-Training) or **EI** (Engineer Intern). The credential is national in practice but conferred by your state.
3. **Accumulate experience under a licensed PE** — typically **4 years** of progressive engineering work. Some states reduce the requirement for advanced degrees; details vary.
4. **Pass the PE exam** — the discipline-specific Principles and Practice of Engineering exam.
5. **Apply to a state licensure board** — provide references, transcripts, experience verification. Upon approval and payment, you are a licensed **Professional Engineer (PE)** in that state.

### 6.2 Reciprocity (comity)

A PE license is issued by a state, not the federal government. To practice in a state where you are not licensed, you typically apply for **comity** (also called reciprocity) — the new state recognizes your existing license after verifying credentials. Comity is not automatic; some states have stricter requirements (additional ethics exam, residency, references). **Plan ahead before working on a project in a state where you are not licensed.**

### 6.3 Continuing education (PDH)

Most states require **Professional Development Hours (PDH)** to maintain licensure — typically 15 PDH per year or 30 over two years, with at least 1–2 PDH in ethics. Failure to complete PDH is the most common cause of administrative license issues. Track and document your hours rigorously.

### 6.4 The "industrial exemption"

In many states, engineers working solely on products or processes manufactured/owned by their employer (and not for external clients or for public works) are exempt from PE licensure requirements. This is the **industrial exemption**, and it explains why most engineers in manufacturing companies are not licensed. The exemption is real but narrow:

- It generally does **not** apply to anyone offering services to the public.
- It generally does **not** apply to anyone whose work affects public health/safety in a regulated context (consulting engineering, civil works, building design).
- It is being narrowed in many states over time.

Even where the exemption applies, you may not call yourself a "Professional Engineer," "PE," or "Engineer" in title in some states unless licensed — the *practice* may be exempt, but the *title* often is not.

### 6.5 Consequences of unlicensed practice

Unlicensed practice where licensure is required can result in: civil fines, injunction against your practice, voiding of contracts and inability to collect fees, professional discipline against any licensed PE who aided you, and in some states criminal misdemeanor charges. Plans you stamped without authority are void; this exposes your client and the public to a totally separate cascade of liability.

## 7. Signing & Sealing

### 7.1 What a PE seal legally means

When you affix your PE seal and signature to an engineering document, you are stating, under penalty of state law:

1. The work was prepared by you, or under your **direct supervision and control**.
2. You are **competent** in the technical discipline of the document.
3. The work conforms to **accepted engineering practice** and applicable codes.
4. You **take personal professional responsibility** for the contents.

The seal converts an engineering opinion into a regulated professional act. It is a legal instrument, not a decoration.

### 7.2 Plan stamping (the prohibition)

"Plan stamping" — sealing a document you did not prepare and did not directly supervise — is per se professional misconduct in every U.S. state. It is one of the fastest paths to license revocation. The defense "I knew the engineer who did the work and trusted them" is not a defense.

**Direct supervision and control** means you reviewed, verified, and could have modified the design. It does not mean you glanced at the cover sheet, or that the work was done by another PE in your firm. The exam-style answer: *if you cannot answer detailed technical questions about the design choices, you did not supervise it, and you cannot seal it.*

### 7.3 When sealing is required

Generally required: civil/structural designs for buildings, bridges, public works; mechanical designs for HVAC, fire-protection systems; electrical designs for buildings; certain land surveys; environmental permits. **Always check the specific state statute** — the requirements are not uniform.

## 8. Intellectual Property

### 8.1 The four categories

| Type | Protects | Duration | How obtained |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Patent (utility)** | Functional inventions — new, useful, non-obvious | 20 years from filing | Apply to USPTO; examined |
| **Patent (design)** | Ornamental appearance | 15 years | Apply to USPTO |
| **Patent (plant)** | Asexually reproduced plant varieties | 20 years | Apply to USPTO |
| **Copyright** | Original expression (drawings, manuals, software code) | Life + 70 years (individual) | Automatic on fixation; registration strengthens |
| **Trademark** | Source-identifying marks (names, logos) | Indefinite (with use) | Common law on use; federal registration via USPTO |
| **Trade secret** | Confidential commercial info with economic value | Indefinite | Reasonable efforts to maintain secrecy |

For mechanical engineers, the day-to-day stack is usually: utility patents (the design itself), trade secrets (manufacturing know-how, vendor lists, internal test data), and copyright (drawings and software).

### 8.2 Work-for-hire and assignment of inventions

If you are a salaried employee whose job involves creating inventions, your employment agreement almost certainly contains an **assignment-of-inventions clause** transferring ownership of your work-product inventions to your employer. This is enforceable in most jurisdictions for inventions:
- Made during work hours, or
- Made using employer resources, or
- Within the scope of your job duties, or
- Relating to your employer's actual or anticipated business.

Some states (notably California, Illinois, Washington, Minnesota) statutorily carve out an employee's right to inventions made **entirely on their own time, with their own resources, unrelated to the employer's business.** Read your specific employment agreement and your state's statute.

### 8.3 Using a prior employer's IP — what's allowed and what isn't

**Allowed:**
- General skills, knowledge, and experience you acquired.
- Information that is genuinely public (published patents, articles, technical literature).
- Your own residual memory of techniques.

**Not allowed:**
- Specific documents, drawings, datasets, or code copied from your prior employer.
- Trade secrets — your duty of confidentiality survives termination.
- Customer lists or confidential commercial info.
- "Inventing" something that closely tracks your prior employer's confidential work.

The bright line: **leave with your brain, not with files.** Returning company materials on departure is non-negotiable. The doctrine of *inevitable disclosure* in some jurisdictions can bar you from a directly competing role even without proof of misappropriation.

## 9. Contracts & Liability

### 9.1 Types of engineering liability

- **Negligence** — failure to exercise the *standard of care* of a reasonably prudent engineer in the same discipline and locale. Most engineering claims are negligence claims. **Standard of care is not perfection** — it is what a competent peer would have done.
- **Breach of contract** — failure to deliver what the contract specifies. Damages typically limited by contract terms (caps, indemnification, exclusions of consequential damages).
- **Breach of warranty** — express warranties (you stated the design would do X) or implied warranties (fitness for a particular purpose, merchantability — mostly relevant when engineering deliverables blur into product warranties).
- **Strict liability** — liability without fault, typically applied to manufactured products with defects (design defect, manufacturing defect, failure to warn). The plaintiff need not prove negligence, only that the product was defective and caused the harm. Mostly applies to product designers, not consulting engineers.
- **Fraud / misrepresentation** — knowing falsehood; can pierce contract liability limits and is uninsurable.

### 9.2 Standard of care vs. "code-compliant" vs. "adequately safe"

A common misconception: "we met code, so we are safe." Code compliance is the **minimum legal floor**, not the ceiling of engineering judgment. Standard of care can require more than code where:
- The risk is well-known to the profession.
- Industry practice exceeds code.
- The specific application has known failure modes not addressed by code.
- A reasonable peer would have done more.

You can be **code-compliant and still negligent** if a reasonably prudent engineer would have done more, and your failure to do so foreseeably caused harm. Conversely, you can be **arguably non-compliant on a technicality but not negligent** if a reasonable peer would have done the same thing under the circumstances — though the regulatory exposure is independent.

### 9.3 Expert witness duties and limits

When testifying as an expert:

- Your duty is to the court (the truth), not to the party who hired you.
- State your qualifications honestly; do not testify outside your area of competence.
- Distinguish opinion from fact; identify the basis of every opinion.
- Disclose your compensation and any prior relationship with the parties.
- Do not advocate; explain.

Fee structures contingent on the outcome of testimony are typically prohibited; flat or hourly fees are standard.

## 10. Sustainability & Environmental Responsibility

A canon-six obligation that has matured from "nice to have" to "expected practice" in the 21st century. The NSPE has incorporated sustainability into its ethics guidance, and the ASCE Code of Ethics now explicitly includes sustainability among its fundamental principles.

Practical implications for the engineer's daily work:

- **Life-cycle thinking** — consider impacts across material extraction, manufacturing, operation, maintenance, end-of-life. Don't optimize one phase at the expense of the whole.
- **Environmental impact disclosure** — when a design has foreseeable environmental impact, surface it to the client/decision-maker even when not legally required.
- **Climate-aware design** — for long-lived infrastructure, design margins should reflect anticipated climate conditions over the asset's life, not the climate of the past.
- **Honest reporting** — do not assist clients in greenwashing or in misrepresenting environmental performance.

A typical FE-style question now treats environmental harm as part of Canon 1's "welfare of the public" — pollution that endangers downstream communities is a public-safety matter, not merely a regulatory matter.

## 11. Honesty in Engineering Communication

The Canon 3 / Professional Obligation cluster. The day-to-day rules:

- **Data integrity** — never fabricate, omit, or selectively present test data. The selective omission is the most common failure mode; the canonical answer is *include all relevant and pertinent information*.
- **Citing sources** — credit the work of others; quotations, derivations, and design ideas from external sources must be attributable.
- **No plagiarism** — copying another engineer's report, calculations, or drawings without attribution is a discipline-level offense.
- **No falsifying credentials** — never overstate education, licensure status, experience, or specialization. The FE-level "trick" question: claiming "PE" before your license is issued is a misrepresentation, even by one day.
- **Non-misleading public statements** — accurate in fact AND in implication. Technical truth that is engineered to mislead is still a violation.

A useful test for a public statement: would a non-specialist reading this come away with a correct impression of the facts? If not — even if every individual sentence is literally true — you have failed Canon 3.

## 12. Bribery and the Foreign Corrupt Practices Act (FCPA)

If you work for a U.S.-based company (or any company with U.S. operations or U.S. capital markets exposure), the **FCPA** prohibits offering, paying, promising, or authorizing anything of value to a foreign government official to obtain or retain business or to secure an improper advantage.

### 12.1 Gifts vs. bribes

- **Gift** — a token given without expectation of specific reciprocal action; modest value; permitted in some contexts under both the giver's and receiver's local rules.
- **Bribe** — anything of value given with intent to influence an official act.

The intent and the magnitude determine the line. A $10 promotional pen given to a regulator is usually fine. A $1,000 watch given to the regulator the week before your permit decision is a bribe regardless of how it is labeled.

### 12.2 "Facilitation payments"

A narrow FCPA exception historically permitted small payments to expedite *routine non-discretionary government actions* (processing a visa, scheduling an inspection that was going to happen anyway). **In practice, most major U.S. companies prohibit facilitation payments entirely** because the line is too easy to cross and the UK Bribery Act and many other jurisdictions criminalize them. Default: do not pay.

### 12.3 Working internationally — due diligence on agents

If you retain a local agent, sales rep, or consultant in a foreign jurisdiction:
- Conduct due diligence on the agent's reputation, beneficial owners, and government connections.
- Contract for FCPA-compliance representations and audit rights.
- Monitor payments — round-number "consulting fees" that exceed market rates are red flags.

You can be liable under FCPA for the acts of your agents if you should have known what they would do with the money.

## 13. Working Outside Your Competence — When to Refuse

Most ethics failures don't begin with malice. They begin with a competent engineer who took on a job that was a half-step too far and rationalized it.

### 13.1 The three-question test

Before accepting an assignment outside your familiar territory, ask:

1. **Can I articulate the failure modes of this system?** If you cannot list the half-dozen ways this could fail, you cannot design it safely.
2. **Do I know what I don't know?** "Conscious incompetence" is workable — you can ask. "Unconscious incompetence" is dangerous — you can't ask the questions you don't know to ask.
3. **Is the consequence of getting this wrong recoverable?** A prototype with low energy density, recoverable. A pressure vessel, a public bridge, an aircraft component — not recoverable.

If the answer to any of the three is unfavorable, the canonical responses (in order):

1. **Bring in a specialist** to design and seal the segment.
2. **Acquire the competence** legitimately before taking the assignment.
3. **Refuse the assignment.**

### 13.2 Supervised practice

Junior engineers grow competence by working under a PE who reviews and accepts responsibility. This is good and necessary. The arrangement breaks down when:
- The supervising PE is not actually reviewing the work (i.e., plan stamping).
- The work is outside the supervising PE's competence too.
- The junior is being told to "just do it" without genuine mentorship.

The supervising PE's name on the seal means *they* answer for the design. If you are a junior and your supervisor is asking you to perform work they cannot meaningfully review, escalate.

## 14. FE-Style Question Patterns — Quick Mapping

| Stem language | Controlling canon / rule |
|---|---|
| "Public safety is endangered…" | Canon 1 |
| "Outside your expertise…" / "discipline…" | Canon 2 |
| "Sign / seal / stamp…" | Canon 2 + Rule on direct supervision |
| "Statement to the press / public…" | Canon 3 |
| "Opinion on a paid topic…" | Canon 3 (disclose interest) |
| "Conflict of interest…" / "gift…" | Canon 4 |
| "Two clients on same project…" | Canon 4 (disclose + consent) |
| "Misleading qualifications / contributions to award…" | Canon 5 |
| "Criticize a competitor…" | Professional Obligations §6, §7 |
| "Credit for design / invention…" | Professional Obligation §9 |
| "Discover an error in prior work…" | Honesty / Professional Obligation §1 |
| "Reveal confidential information…" | Default no; YES if needed to protect public (Canon 1 override) |

### 14.1 What the correct answer almost always is

When in doubt on an FE/PE ethics question, the safe defaults — which match the exam-key answer in the overwhelming majority of cases — are:

1. **Disclose** (everything: conflicts, errors, paid interests, dual roles).
2. **Decline** (gifts, work outside competence, dual representation without consent).
3. **Document** (in writing, with dates, when management overrules you).
4. **Refuse to sign** anything you didn't prepare or supervise, or that endangers the public.
5. **Notify the authority** (board, regulator) when internal escalation fails on a safety matter.
6. **Public safety wins.** Always. When two canons appear to conflict, choose the one that protects the public.

If your gut answer is "stay quiet to protect my career / my employer / the contract," it is almost certainly the wrong answer.

## 15. Common Student Pitfalls

- **Placing client loyalty above public safety.** Canon 4 is *subordinate* to Canon 1. Faithful agency ends where public danger begins. The FE exam tests this exact pattern relentlessly.
- **Conflating "legal" with "ethical."** Code-compliant designs can be unethically deficient. Legally permitted gifts can be unethically accepted. The Code sets a higher bar than the law in many places.
- **Believing "I was told to" is a defense.** It isn't. Your seal is yours; your judgment is yours; your liability is yours.
- **Believing "I didn't know" is a defense.** Canon 2 turns "I didn't know" into a competence violation. You were obliged to know — or to refuse the work.
- **Treating apparent COIs as harmless if no actual conflict exists.** The Code requires disclosure of apparent and potential COIs too. Appearance matters.
- **Reading "disclose" as "tell your boss" only.** Canon 1's public-safety override may require disclosure to authorities outside your employer when your employer is the source of the danger.
- **Assuming the industrial exemption broadly covers them.** It is narrow. When in doubt, get licensed.
- **Confusing the standard of care with perfection.** You are not negligent for being wrong; you are negligent for falling below what a reasonable peer would have done.
- **Underestimating the seal.** Engineers occasionally sign documents as a favor or out of social pressure. Every sealed document is a personal legal commitment. Treat it accordingly.
- **Sycophantic loyalty mistaken for "faithful agency."** Canon 4 asks you to be a trustee — which includes telling your client the hard truth, declining to do unwise work, and disclosing risk. A "faithful agent" who only tells the client what they want to hear is failing the canon.
- **"It's just a small change" reasoning.** Hyatt Regency, Citicorp, and many other disasters started with a small change. Treat every change to a load path, a control system, a safety interlock as a fresh engineering decision requiring re-analysis.

## 16. The Engineer's Internal Compass — A Three-Question Pre-Decision Test

When facing a situation that "feels off" and the canon mapping isn't immediately obvious:

1. **The public test:** Would I be comfortable explaining this decision to the people who will use the thing I'm designing — without omitting anything?
2. **The board test:** Would I be comfortable explaining this decision to my state licensure board's discipline panel?
3. **The front-page test:** Would I be comfortable reading about this decision on the front page of tomorrow's paper, with my name attached?

Three "yes" answers, proceed. Any "no," stop and re-examine.

These tests are not in the NSPE Code. They are tools to surface what the Code's spirit already requires.
